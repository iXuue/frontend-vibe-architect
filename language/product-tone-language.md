# Product Tone Language

## Use When

Use when the user describes the product personality, audience feeling, brand posture, or emotional impression.

## What User Might Say

- It should feel trustworthy.
- Make it young and energetic.
- I want it serious and professional.
- It should feel friendly but not childish.
- Make it suitable for enterprise users.
- I want it to feel creative and alive.

## What It May Mean

- Brand voice should shape visual hierarchy, color, typography, imagery, and interaction style.
- Different audiences need different density, restraint, and feedback.
- Tone should match product risk: finance, health, and enterprise tools usually need more confidence than spectacle.
- Creative tools can tolerate more expressive motion and visual personality than operational dashboards.

## What It Does Not Necessarily Mean

- Trustworthy does not mean boring.
- Friendly does not mean cartoon-like.
- Enterprise does not mean gray and lifeless.
- Young does not mean chaotic.
- Creative does not mean low usability.

## Clarifying Questions

- Who needs to trust this product?
- What should the product never feel like?
- Is the tone for first impression, daily use, or both?
- Are there existing brand assets, colors, screenshots, or references?

## Design Variables To Adjust

- Color temperature and saturation.
- Typography personality.
- Illustration or image style.
- Component roundness and density.
- Voice of labels and empty states.
- Motion expressiveness.
- Error and success feedback tone.

## Suitable Tracks

- `tracks/landing-brand.md` when tone drives conversion and storytelling.
- `tracks/web-product.md` when tone must support product use.
- `tracks/mobile-direction.md` when tone affects repeated personal interaction.
- `tracks/desktop-direction.md` when tone must stay stable for focused work.

## Suitable Modes

- `modes/simple-designed.md` for serious, trustworthy, calm, or enterprise tone.
- `modes/dynamic-future.md` for memorable, creative, expressive, or launch-oriented tone when usability allows it.

## Risks

- Letting brand mood override task clarity.
- Choosing a tone that conflicts with user risk or product category.
- Overusing playful details in professional workflows.
- Making enterprise tone too generic.

## Output Guidance

- Translate tone into visual and interaction decisions.
- State which tone is primary and which is secondary.
- Identify tone conflicts, such as "serious but playful".
- Connect tone to audience and product context.
